Money 101 — What is money, really?

8 min read · +30 XP · 3 quizzes

Most people interact with money every day but rarely stop to ask: what is it, fundamentally? Money is a store of value, a medium of exchange, and a unit of account. What makes it valuable is not gold in a vault -- it is trust. The trust that the Rs 500 note in your hand will buy chai tomorrow just as it does today.

Key Concept -- Time Value of Money

Rs 1,000 today is worth more than Rs 1,000 a year from now -- because today's money can be invested to earn returns. This single idea underlies all of finance. Every interest rate, every valuation, every EMI calculation flows from it.

Why keeping cash is a losing game

If inflation in India runs at ~6% per year and your savings account gives you 3%, you are losing 3% of your real purchasing power every year. On Rs 5 lakh in savings, that is Rs 15,000 quietly disappearing -- not from your account, but from what your money can actually buy.

Real-Life Analogy

Think of money like ice. If you just hold it, it melts (inflation). Put it to work -- in an ice machine (investments) -- and it keeps producing more. The goal is not to save ice, it is to build the machine.

The purchasing power trap

Your father's first salary in 1990 might have been Rs 3,000. Today that would barely cover a week's groceries. That is purchasing power erosion. Wealth creation is not about earning more, it is about making your money grow faster than inflation.

Inflation — The silent tax on wealth

7 min read · +30 XP · 2 quizzes

Inflation is the general rise in prices over time. When inflation is 6%, a Rs 100 grocery basket today costs Rs 106 next year. Your money did not change -- but its purchasing power shrank. India's Consumer Price Index (CPI) measures this for a basket of goods a typical household buys.

India Context

India's CPI inflation has averaged ~5-6% over the past decade. Food inflation can spike much higher -- 8-10% in bad monsoon years. Your savings need to outrun this, not just match it.

Real vs Nominal Returns

A 7% FD sounds great -- until you realise 6% went to fighting inflation. Your real return is just 1%. Now add tax: FD interest is taxed at your income slab (30% for top earners), leaving you with a negative real return. This is the inflation trap most salaried professionals fall into.

Savings Account3%2.1%-3.9%

Equity MF (10yr avg)13%11%+5.0%
Think of it this way

Inflation is a treadmill that never stops. Stand still (cash) and you fall behind. Walk (FD) and you barely stay put. You need to run (equity) to actually move forward in real terms.

Investing Basics · Module 03

Compounding — The 8th wonder of the world

10 min read · +40 XP · 3 quizzes

Compounding is earning returns on your returns. Start with Rs 1 lakh at 12% per year: Year 1 gives you Rs 12,000. Year 2, that Rs 1.12 lakh earns Rs 13,440 -- not Rs 12,000. The base keeps growing. Over 25 years, your Rs 1 lakh becomes Rs 17 lakh without adding a single rupee more.

The Rule of 72

Divide 72 by your annual return rate to find how many years it takes to double your money. At 12% — 72 divided by 12 = 6 years to double. At 6% (FD) — 12 years to double. The difference is enormous over a lifetime.

Why starting early is everything

Priya invests Rs 5,000/month from age 22 to 32 (10 years), then stops -- total invested: Rs 6 lakh. Rahul starts at 32 and invests Rs 5,000/month for 28 years until 60 -- total invested: Rs 16.8 lakh. At 12% returns, Priya ends up with more money. You cannot buy back time.

Mutual Funds — Investing made accessible

12 min read · +35 XP · 3 quizzes

A mutual fund pools money from thousands of investors and invests it in stocks, bonds, or both -- managed by a professional fund manager. Instead of picking stocks yourself, you buy units of a fund that already owns a diversified portfolio. Regulated by SEBI. Invest from as little as Rs 500.

Direct vs Regular Plans -- This Matters

Regular Plans go through a distributor or bank who earns commission (0.5-1% extra expense ratio). Direct Plans cut the middleman -- same fund, same manager, ~1% lower cost per year. Over 20 years on Rs 50L corpus, that is roughly Rs 20-25 lakh more in your pocket.

SIP vs Lump Sum

SIP (Systematic Investment Plan) means investing a fixed amount every month. The benefit: you buy more units when markets are low and fewer when high -- rupee cost averaging. Most retail investors are better served by SIP over lump sum.

Investing Basics · Module 05

Stocks vs FD — The real risk conversation

10 min read · +40 XP

Fixed Deposits feel safe. Your principal is guaranteed, the return is locked in. But after tax and inflation, FD real returns are often negative. The question is not "is FD risky?" -- it is "can you afford the risk of not growing your wealth?"

Risk is Not Volatility

Most people confuse volatility (short-term price swings) with actual risk (permanent loss of capital). A Nifty 50 index fund may fall 30% in a crash -- but historically recovers and reaches new highs. The real risk is selling in panic and locking in the loss permanently.

FeatureFDEquity (Long-Term)
Return (10yr avg)6-7%12-14% (Nifty)
After Tax4.2-4.9% (30% slab)10-12% (10% LTCG)
Real Return-1 to 0%+4 to 6%
Principal SafetyGuaranteedMarket-linked
LiquidityModerate (penalty for early break)High (T+1 settlement)

The hybrid approach

The answer is not FD or equity -- it is both, in the right proportions. Emergency fund: liquid MF or FD. Goals under 3 years: debt MF. Goals over 7 years: equity MF or direct stocks. Time horizon is the key variable, not just risk appetite.

Tax Basics — Keep more of what you earn

12 min read · +45 XP

India has two income tax regimes: Old Regime (with deductions like 80C, HRA, home loan interest) and New Regime (lower slab rates, most deductions gone). Neither is universally better -- it depends on your deductions and income level.

SlabOld RegimeNew Regime (FY25)
Up to Rs 3LNilNil
Rs 3L - 7L5%5%
Rs 7L - 10L20%10%
Rs 10L - 12L30%15%
Rs 12L - 15L30%20%
Above Rs 15L30%30%
Capital Gains Tax on Investments

LTCG: Equity held more than 1 year — 10% on gains above Rs 1 lakh/year. STCG: Equity held less than 1 year — 15%. This makes long-term equity tax-efficient vs FD which is taxed at your full income slab rate.

Section 80C -- The Rs 1.5L Deduction

Under the Old Regime, 80C lets you deduct Rs 1.5 lakh from taxable income via ELSS funds, PPF, EPF, or life insurance premiums. For someone in the 30% slab, that is Rs 46,800 saved annually. ELSS has the shortest lock-in (3 years) and market-linked upside -- often the best 80C option for wealth builders.

Goal-Based Investing — Money with a purpose

10 min read · +50 XP

Random investing leads to random outcomes. Goal-based investing reverses the question: start with what you need and when, then work backwards to how much to invest today.

The Three Buckets Framework

Bucket 1 — Safety (0-2 yrs): Emergency fund, near-term goals. Liquid MF, FD.
Bucket 2 — Growth (3-7 yrs): Child's education, car. Balanced or Hybrid MF.
Bucket 3 — Wealth (7+ yrs): Retirement, financial freedom. Pure equity, direct stocks.

The mistake most people make is treating all money as one undifferentiated pool. When markets fall, they panic-sell equity meant for retirement because they needed liquidity for a 2-year goal they had not ring-fenced. The three buckets prevent this entirely.

FIRE — Financial Independence, Retire Early

The 4% rule says: if you can live on 4% of your invested corpus per year, your money will likely outlast you. Flipped around — your FI number is 25× your annual expenses (1 / 0.04 = 25). Some prefer a more conservative 33× multiple for Indian context where equity premium is harder to count on.

FIRE is not about retiring at 35 to a beach. It is about removing financial coercion from your career choices — the ability to say no, to walk away, to take the risky bet, to spend a year with your child. That option has compounding value of its own.

A note on the assumptions

The 4% withdrawal rule was derived from US historical data (1926-1995, mostly bonds + S&P 500). For India, equity premium has been higher but so has inflation. A more conservative 3.5% withdrawal (28× multiple) is defensible — we let you toggle this. We also do not account for healthcare cost inflation, which exceeds general CPI by 3-4 percentage points historically. Use this calculator as a directional anchor, not a precise plan.
